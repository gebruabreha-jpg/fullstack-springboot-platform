Europe is really two coffee markets
1. Commodity / industrial coffee (largest volume)
Buyers:
huge roasters,
supermarket brands,
instant coffee producers.

Think:
espresso blends,
supermarket beans,
capsules.

They optimize for:
price + consistency + supply reliability
Coffee often comes from:
Brazil
Vietnam
Uganda

This is difficult for a new exporter.

Margins are thinner and volumes are large.

2. Specialty coffee (your likely opportunity)

Buyers:

specialty roasters,
boutique importers,
cafés roasting their own beans.

They optimize for:

flavor + story + traceability + uniqueness

Coffee often comes from:

Ethiopia
Colombia
Kenya
Panama

Ethiopia is strong here because buyers actively want:

Yirgacheffe floral coffees,
Sidama fruit-forward coffees,
Guji naturals,
heirloom varieties.









Good question. Ethiopia is included, but usually not as a dominant player in the industrial/commodity segment.

The reason is mostly economics and supply structure.

1. Ethiopia produces premium Arabica, not cheap bulk coffee

Industrial buyers optimize for:

cheap + consistent + huge volume

They want coffee for:

supermarket blends,
capsules,
instant coffee,
large espresso chains.

Typical sourcing logic:

“Give me 5,000 tons of consistent coffee at stable price.”

That favors:

Brazil → massive scale, predictable supply
Vietnam → low-cost robusta
Uganda → competitive robusta

Ethiopian coffee is often sold at a premium because of flavor and reputation.

Industrial buyers may think:

“Why pay Ethiopian prices if this coffee gets blended and roasted dark anyway?”

2. Ethiopia’s supply structure is fragmented

In Ethiopia, coffee often comes from millions of smallholders.

That means:

many tiny farms,
cooperative aggregation,
washing stations,
variable lot sizes.

Industrial buyers prefer:

“one supplier, massive predictable shipment, every month”

Countries like Brazil excel here.

You can buy enormous consistent lots.

3. Ethiopian coffee has strong differentiation

Ethiopia competes on:

uniqueness

not:

lowest cost

Example:

A specialty buyer might say:

“I specifically want a floral washed Yirgacheffe.”

That flavor profile is hard to replace.

An industrial buyer may say:

“I need a reliable Arabica base for espresso blend.”

Different buying logic.

4. Ethiopia still appears in industrial blends — just differently

This is the nuance.

Ethiopian coffee is sometimes used:

as a premium component in blends,
to add aroma/fruit/floral notes,
in higher-end espresso products.

For example:

70% Brazil
20% Colombia
10% Ethiopia

Ethiopia acts as the “character” coffee.

But it is less common as:

the cheap high-volume backbone
